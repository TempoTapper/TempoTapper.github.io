Week 6: Detailed Design

Oct 16-22, 2023

The first phase was figuring what components were needed. These components ended up being: Finger Sleeves, a Microcontroller, a Microcontroller mount, Different sensors (touch sensors), and a Power Source.

Finger sleeves: research was done to figure out the ideal sizing and dimension for the sleeves as well as what fabric or material would be ideal.

Microcontroller: During this week numerous Microcontrollers were investigated based on how well they met our project constraints and criteria with the main factors being size (since this is a weable), number of IO pins, and power consumption.

The Microcontroller Mount: during this phase, the mount itself was not designed, but was just decided to be a component of our device. More specifically, it was decided to be a wrist mount to help keep the ROM of users at a maximum.

Different Sensors: Analysis was done on different touch sensors that would be useful for this device. The touch sensor that was chosen was the piezoelectric touch sensor.

Power Source: Until the controller was chosen along with the different sensors, this component could not be decided on yet.

Week 4: Conceptual Designs, Patent Search and Design Selection

Oct 2-8, 2023

This week our team focused on coming up with conceptual designs. We came up with four designs and they are shown in the figure below

  • Design 1: A flat pad that has pressure sensors layed out in the shape of a hand, where each pressure sensor lines up with the tips of your fingers. The device records various pieces of information including if its pressed, and the duration, which can be converted into various musical concepts like key and rhythm using our software
  • Design 2: A similar approach to design #1, except that it is shaped like a computer mouse, which leaves your hand in a ball shape, which feels more normal and ergonomic
  • Design 3: This design consists of sleeves that are worn over each finger. The sleeves have touch sensitive pads as well, allowing the user to assume whatever position they feel most comfortable in when using the device.

Week 3: Objectives, Goals, Criteria, Constraints

Sep 25-Oct 1, 2023

In week 3, our team focused on clearly defining our goals and objectives for the problem. After interpreting all the data we gathered, we came up with the following goal:

"Design a cheap, ergonomic, and interactive solution for music students to practice their musical rhythm/tempo while obtaining objective feedback. The solution should include a physical device capable of tracking hand/finger movements, analyzing the rhythm/tempo with respect to the input, and returning the objective feedback via software interface."

Week 2: User Surveys & Data Collection

Sep 18-24, 2023

In the second week, our team focused on gathering data and asking questions to better focus on the need. We interview music professors, students, and friends asking them various questions relating to learning rhythm.

Some of the responses included:

  • Having an objective form of feedback would be great for learning different rhythms
  • Could be used in classrooms as a visual aid to learning rhythm and a more interactive approach
  • Could have use cases for converting gestures into music
  • Could have use cases as a more ergonomic way to generate MIDI patterns.

After interviewing a few professors who teach topics ranging from music interpretation to music theory, it is clear that a device like this is not currently used in classrooms, and that it would accelerate learning and improvement.

Week 1: Problem Formulation

Sep 11-17, 2023

During the first week, our team was focused on honing in on a specific problem and ensuring there was a problem to solve. We initially had multiple potential ideas, but through meeting with professors, we were able to hone in and choose one. Through our initial research and experience, we found that there was area for improvement in teaching students rhythm. We researched the problem more, and ultimately came up with the following problem:

"There is a need for a cheaper, more ergonomic, and interactive solution for music students to learn rhythm/tempo that includes a form of objective feedback. ​Currently, most of the learning feedback relies on the music instructor's own judgment for determining if they are correct".

During the problem formulation's intial states, our group had some issues focusing specifically on the need and target audience, and this was to be addressed in the following week.
